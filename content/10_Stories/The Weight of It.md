## Scene: The Wallwalk

_Late evening. Snow drifts quietly across the battlements of Castle Kharne._

The training field below lies empty, the churned snow frozen hard again.

Two figures sit on the edge of the wallwalk, boots dangling over the drop.

[[Blue Sylvanranth]] and [[Katherine Kendal]] huddle in winter cloaks. The castle is silent around them.

It feels like the whole place is holding its breath.

Blue stares down at the frozen training yard. Her usual brightness has gone flat.

Kendal notices.

She’s seen this look before.

Kendal: You’re doing it again.

Blue: Doing what?

Kendal: The thing where you replay the fight in your head like you’re gonna change the ending.

Blue: Maybe I am.

Kendal pulls a flask from her belt and offers it.

Blue takes a sip.

She grimaces.

Blue: Still terrible.

Kendal: You keep hoping it’ll get better. Like the next sip might be warm cider instead of winter regret.

Blue: Maybe next sip I forget what their eyes looked like.

The words hang in the cold air.

Kendal doesn’t dodge them.

Kendal: They were goblins.

Blue: They were scared. They bled. I made them stop bleeding.

Kendal: I watched a slaver take a lance to the face when I was twelve. Sat through a bandit execution a year later. Everyone acts surprised when people die—like the world wasn’t already ending for them.

Blue: Doesn’t mean it’s not supposed to hurt.

Kendal leans back against the stone, watching the stars come out.

Kendal: If it hurts every time, you don’t make it long.

Blue keeps staring down at the frozen field.

Blue: I’ve been thinking about the ones that just… disappeared. Back in Bard’s Gate. Halfbloods mostly. Good people. Loud laughs. Stupid songs.

She swallows.

Blue: There was this boy. Big ears. Bigger heart. First crush, maybe.

A faint smile flickers.

Blue: He used to sneak me raisins behind the bar. Said he’d open a fruit cart one day. Get out of the city.

The smile fades.

Blue: He got stabbed in an alley. No one found him for three days.

A long pause.

Blue: I didn’t even notice he was gone.

Her voice softens.

Blue: I just smiled and danced and… the hitchhiker made everything bright, even when it wasn’t.

Kendal studies her quietly.

Kendal: You miss him now.

Blue: I miss the people I never even thought to miss.

A slow breath leaves her.

Blue: And I’m scared one day I’ll kill someone, and I won’t feel anything at all.

Kendal doesn’t answer immediately.

Instead she unwinds the scarf from her neck and drapes it around Blue’s shoulders.

Kendal: If that day comes, I’ll remind you.

Blue nods.

Blue: You do the same for me?

Kendal: Always.

They sit there until the cold finally drives them back inside.

Below them, the field waits.

Above them, near the shadowed tower stairs, someone watches unseen.

[[Dana Freeblade]].

She stands half-turned in the darkness, one hand covering her mouth.

## Scene: Iron Beneath the Ice

_Castle Kharne — Lower Armory Hall, early morning._

Cold light cuts through the arrow slits, catching on polished steel.

[[Beringer]] stands before a rack of longswords, methodically checking each blade.

The door opens.

[[Dana Freeblade]] steps inside.

She stops just inside the threshold.

Dana: I saw Kendal with Blue last night. After drills.

Beringer keeps working the blade in his hand.

Beringer: She trains with her. That’s not news.

Dana: No. She stayed with her. On the wallwalk.

A pause.

Dana: Long after the others went in.

Beringer slides the sword back onto the rack.

Dana folds her arms.

Dana: Blue was spiraling.

Dana: Kendal didn’t flinch.

Another pause.

Dana: I’ve underestimated her.

She steps further into the room.

Dana: I thought she was all grooming and good posture. Turns out she’s better steel than I gave her credit for.

Beringer glances over.

Beringer: She’s my squire for a reason.

Dana: And that reason’s clearer now.

Dana’s tone stays measured.

Dana: She held the line for Blue just by being there. That takes more grit than people think.

She meets his eyes.

Dana: You and I don’t agree on much. But I can recognize good work.

A pause.

Dana: Kendal did good work last night.

Beringer says nothing.

Dana nods once.

Dana: I figured you’d want to hear it from someone who doesn’t hand out praise lightly.

She turns and leaves the armory.

Beringer doesn’t stop her.

After she’s gone, he rests a hand on one of the blades.

Just for a moment.

Maybe thinking. Maybe not.

## Historical Context

By the winter of **1230 OR**, the Maurakappi larva had already been removed from [[Blue Sylvanranth]].

Physically she was considered healed.

The pheromones the creature had exuded for years were gone, along with the subtle emotional haze that had surrounded her since her time in Bard’s Gate. Those effects had shaped not only how others reacted to Blue, but how she moved through the world herself.

People had always found it difficult to stay angry with her. Arguments drifted away. Harsh words softened before they landed. Even those inclined toward cruelty often found their temper slipping away in her presence.

With the larva removed, that strange advantage vanished overnight.

For the first time in years, people reacted to Blue normally.

She noticed immediately.

The other change was quieter.

The creature had not simply dulled her mind, but it had encouraged a strange emotional buoyancy — a constant brightness that bordered on giddy optimism. Blue had been cheerful almost to a fault: quick laughter, easy smiles, a stubborn refusal to dwell on darker thoughts.

With the chemical influence gone, that effortless joy faded.

What remained was the habit of it.

Blue was still outwardly irrepressible — still quick with jokes, still smiling, still bouncing back from setbacks with apparent ease. But those closest to her began to notice that the cheerfulness had become something slightly different.

Less instinct.

More performance.

The reflex of someone who had spent years teaching herself to stay bright no matter how dark things became.

Her mind itself seemed clearer as well. The faint dreamlike quality that had clung to her for so long was gone, replaced by sharper focus and quicker insight. Friends joked that Blue had “gained a point of wit overnight,” though the truth was simpler: the fog had lifted.

What no one yet understood was that the larva had already served its deeper purpose.

During the years it lived within her, agents of the [[Dominion]] had layered psychological conditioning around Blue’s identity, loyalties, and sense of duty.

The chemical influence had faded.

The deeper alterations remained.

So when Blue sat on the wallwalk that winter night and confessed that she feared one day she might kill someone and feel nothing…

…it was not simply the anxiety of a young knight confronting the cost of violence.

It was the first conscious glimpse of something that had already begun.
