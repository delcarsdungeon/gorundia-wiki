The hall outside the chapel was quiet in the late afternoon, the kind of stillness that settles over stone buildings when the day’s visitors have mostly gone and the candles are being trimmed for evening prayers.

Dame Cwenheld sat near one of the tall windows where the light was warmest.

She no longer read much—her eyes made that difficult—but she liked the sun on her face. The chair was heavy oak, carved with the lightning bolt of Heironeous. A plain white mantle rested over her shoulders, heavy enough to soften the worst of the scarring along her neck and collar.

Her helmet rested nearby on the bench.

The attached wig—long grey hair meant to soften the shock of her burns—had been carefully combed.

She heard the footsteps before the knock.

Young footsteps. Hesitant.

Cwenheld turned her head slightly.

“Come in,” she said.

The door opened slowly. Three young people stepped inside, uncertain whether they truly belonged there.

A young man in a traveler’s cloak.
A girl clutching a basket to her chest.
A boy hanging back behind them, trying very hard to look brave.

For a moment none of them spoke.

Cwenheld shifted in her chair, suddenly aware of them staring.

She reached instinctively toward the bench for her helmet.

“Ah—just a moment,” she murmured.

Her fingers brushed the edge of the helm, trying to lift it so she could pull the mask and wig down over her ruined face.

The movement was clumsy.

Her damaged vision betrayed her.

The helmet slipped from her grasp and clattered to the stone floor.

The sound echoed through the quiet chamber.

For a brief moment no one moved.

Cwenheld sighed softly.

“Well,” she muttered dryly, “that could have gone more gracefully.”

The youngest of the three stepped forward immediately.

He picked up the helmet with careful hands, holding it as though it were a sacred object rather than a piece of battered armor.

He hesitated a moment before offering it back to her.

Up close they could see her clearly now.

The burn scars that crossed her cheek and jaw.  
The pale, watery eyes that did not quite focus where they should.

None of them recoiled.

The girl stepped closer and gently placed the helmet into Cwenheld’s hands.

“There you are, my lady,” she said quietly.

Cwenheld held the helm for a moment.

She could feel the familiar weight of it. The wig brushed against her wrist.

Slowly she lifted it toward her head.

Then she paused.

The three young visitors were still standing there, watching her with something that was not pity.

It was reverence.

After a moment Cwenheld lowered the helmet again.

She turned it over in her hands, considering.

Then she set it aside on the bench.

“Well,” she said, settling back into the chair, “you’ve already seen the worst of it.”

The tension in the room broke slightly.

The girl stepped forward.

“My lady… Dame Cwenheld?”

“That is the name I answer to.”

The girl knelt quickly and kissed the hem of the white mantle draped across the knight’s lap.

The other two followed her lead.

Cwenheld stiffened slightly.

“Oh now—none of that,” she said gently, reaching down to lift them. “Stand up. You’ll wrinkle the cloth.”

But the girl looked up with shining eyes.

“I was a child at Wycliffe Ford,” she said quickly. “My father drove one of the wagons in the caravan. When the bandits came… you stood in the bridge.”

Cwenheld went very still.

The girl opened the basket she had been clutching.

Inside were small loaves of bread wrapped in linen.

“My mother bakes now,” she said. “She told me if I ever came through the capital I had to bring these to you. She says she still remembers the sound your shield made when the arrows hit it.”

The young man beside her cleared his throat.

“I was at Marrow Field,” he said. “Not a soldier. Just… a farm boy. My sisters were running for the gates.”

He glanced down at Cwenheld’s scarred hands.

“You rode past me,” he said. “Straight at the ogres. I thought you were mad.”

A faint crooked smile touched the knight’s mouth.

“That is a popular theory.”

The boy behind them finally stepped forward.

He unfolded a small cloth bundle.

Inside was a battered shard of iron shaped roughly like a lightning bolt.

“My grandmother told me to give you this,” he said. “It broke off your shield at the Chapel of Broken Spears. She kept it all these years.”

He held it out carefully.

Cwenheld took the fragment and turned it slowly in her palm.

Her fingers traced the old burn marks in the metal.

“I had wondered where that piece went,” she murmured.

The young man shifted awkwardly.

“We just wanted to thank you.”

The girl nodded.

“You saved us.”

They bowed again—less formally this time—and quietly left the chamber.

When the door closed the room grew still once more.

Cwenheld sat there for a long time with the broken shard resting in her hand.

Her helmet sat beside her on the bench.

Unworn.

Finally she let out a slow breath.

“Theravain,” she murmured softly to the empty room.

“You old bastard.”

A faint smile crossed her scarred face.

“They remember.”
