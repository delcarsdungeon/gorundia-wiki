## Sorek’s Letter

Kat didn’t read it right away.

Sorek had folded the paper once more than necessary, thumb worrying the crease like he wasn’t sure he should be handing it over at all.

He didn’t make a speech. Just cleared his throat, looked anywhere but at her face, and said:

> “Didn’t feel right letting it sit in my pocket.”

That was it.

Kat took it with both hands, like it might bruise if she didn’t.  
She didn’t joke. Didn’t deflect.

Just nodded once — the kind of nod soldiers use when they understand exactly what’s being asked of them.

---

Later, much later, she was sitting with Elizavetta on the low wall outside the guest wing.

No music reached that far.  
Just the hum of the city settling into night and the distant clink of armor being set aside.

Kat stared at the folded paper for a while before speaking.

> “He wrote this for his daughter,” she said finally.

Elizavetta didn’t ask which one.

Kat handed it over.

---

Elizavetta read slowly.

Once, straight through.

Then again, stopping here and there, thumb pressing the page flat when the wind tried to take it.

Her expression didn’t change much — but her shoulders eased in small, almost invisible increments, like armor being unbuckled one strap at a time.

When she finished, she folded it back exactly the way it had been given.

> “That’s not a man who thinks marriage is a fairy tale,” Elizavetta said quietly.

> “No,” Kat replied. “That’s a man who thinks it’s a bridge you either build right or drown under.”

They sat with that for a bit.

---

“I don’t want any of this,” Kat added, gesturing vaguely toward the lights, the halls, the unseen dance floors and smiling teeth.

“The watching. The measuring. I didn’t earn my place by being… evaluated.”

Elizavetta glanced at her sideways.

> “And yet,” she said gently, “he trusted you with it.”

Kat snorted.

> “Lucky me.”

> “No,” Elizavetta said. “Trusted you not to use it.”

That landed harder.

---

Kat swallowed, then reached out and tucked the paper back into her inner pocket — the one closest to her ribs.

Not hidden.

Just… kept.

> “I’m not going to play their game,” she said.

Elizavetta nodded.

> “You don’t have to.”

A beat.

> “But knowing the rules,” Elizavetta continued, “lets you decide when to flip the table.”

Kat huffed a quiet laugh.

> “You’re getting good at this court thing.”

Elizavetta smiled — small, real.

> “I learned from people who survived worse.”

---

They sat there until the night cooled, the paper warm against Kat’s chest.

Not a guide she wanted.  
Not a future she’d chosen.

But advice, given honestly.

And grief, shared sideways — the only way either of them seemed to know how.

Somewhere inside, music swelled.

Neither of them moved to join it.
