## History

---
The tide came in under gray canvas.

Low, broad-beamed cutters slid toward the shingle with oars muffled in leather. No painted prows. No roaring theatrics. Just men in dark mail and boiled leather, shields lacquered plain, helms unadorned. Professionals.

Smoke rose first—tight columns from signal barns along the coast—then the distant crack of a watchtower bell cut short.

Baron Kaelric Vorthal was already riding when the second bell died.

He had a dozen with him—border patrol, light mail, no banner flying. Only he and his squire were mounted; the rest had marched the ridgeline to keep the shore in sight. When they crested the bluff, they saw the landing party fanning out with practiced spacing.

Too many.

The sergeant’s voice stayed level. “My lord. You and the boy can break through. Ravenhold needs warning. We’ll buy the time.”

Kaelric’s red cloak snapped once in the wind and settled.

He leaned down from the saddle and clasped the sergeant’s shoulder—hard, grateful.

Then he dismounted.

“I stand with my men.”

He didn’t raise his voice. He didn’t have to.

For a heartbeat he rested his brow against his horse’s. The animal blew warm breath into the cold air. Kaelric’s gloved hand lingered at its cheek.

“Run true,” he whispered.

He pressed the reins into his squire’s trembling grip.

“Ride like the wind. Don’t look back. Sound the alert. Tell them we held.”

“My lord—”

“That’s an order.”

The boy wheeled and spurred downslope, hooves hammering through frost and stone, vanishing into the treeline.

Kaelric turned back to the advancing line—shields lifting in unison, crossbows coming up behind them.

He drew his sword. Clean steel caught the light.

Around him, Ravenster infantry locked shields.

The enemy advanced at a measured pace, silent but for boots on gravel.

Baron Kaelric Vorthal stepped forward to close the gap between himself and his footmen.

And as the first volley snapped toward them, he smiled—bright, fearless, almost boyish—while the line braced for impact.
