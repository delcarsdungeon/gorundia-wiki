# Oeridian Tribes

> “The plains are ours, the forests belong to the spirits, and the mountains belong to monsters.”  
> — old Oeridian saying, preserved in later Gorundian chronicles

Long before the founding of Gorundia, the lands that now form the kingdom were held by scattered **Oeridian tribes**. These peoples were not a single nation, nor even a stable confederation, but a broad family of clans, horse-bands, and hillfort communities spread across the plains and river valleys west of the [[Baklhaut Mountains]].

They were the first enduring human culture to dominate the open country of the region.

Though later generations would remember them as “tribal,” the Oeridians of this age were neither primitive nor directionless. They possessed strong traditions of kinship, horsemanship, raiding, oath-making, and seasonal migration. Their world was bounded by enemies and mysteries on every side, and that pressure shaped them into a hard and mobile people.

---

## The Lands of the Early Oeridians

The early Oeridian tribes occupied the **open plains, broad river valleys, and low rolling country** that now make up the heart of Gorundia.

Their lands were naturally defined by stronger powers around them:

- to the **west**, the deep forests belonged to elves, druids, and stranger things older than men  
- to the **east**, the mountains and hills were contested by orc tribes and dwarven holds  
- to the **north**, the open grazing country gave way to harder riding peoples who would one day become the ancestors of the [[Qazaq Nomads]]  
- to the **south and southeast**, later generations would encounter refugee Suel settlements and, eventually, the rising proto-Dominion

This geography produced a culture that understood one lesson very clearly:

the plains could be held, but not enclosed.

---

## A Horse People of the Plains

The Oeridian tribes of this era were a **horse-using pastoral people**.

Their communities tended to be built around:

- extended kin groups
- warbands tied by oath
- seasonal grazing patterns
- small fortified settlements on defensible rises

They raised horses, cattle, and sheep, and they moved with the rhythms of pasture, weather, and raiding season. Some tribes wandered more widely; others had already begun to develop semi-permanent hillforts and seasonal market sites.

In war they favored:

- fast riders
- spear levies
- shield-bearing clan retinues
- chiefs surrounded by household champions

They were not yet a kingdom, but the foundations of later Gorundian cavalry culture were already present.

---

## Hillforts and Clan Law

Though much of their identity was mobile, the Oeridians were not rootless.

Many tribes maintained **hillfort settlements** or enclosed high-ground camps that served as:

- storehouses
- winter refuges
- meeting places for clan councils
- sacred sites for oath-taking and burial

Power rested less on formal title than on reputation.

Chiefs ruled by:

- proven courage
- generosity in feast and plunder
- success in war
- the loyalty of kin and sworn companions

A weak chief could be ignored, challenged, or abandoned.

A strong one might hold several clans together for a generation.

This political instability made the tribes difficult to conquer, but it also made lasting unity rare.

---

## The Western Boundary

To the west lay the deep forests.

The Oeridians did not consider those woods truly theirs.

Early contact with the elves taught them caution. Hunters who crossed too far beneath the deep canopy sometimes returned with stories of silent wardens, warning arrows, strange songs in the dusk, and paths that no longer led where they should.

Over time a cultural boundary formed.

Among the tribes it became common wisdom that the western forests belonged to:

- long-lived folk
- old spirits
- gods best left unbothered

This did not mean peace in every age, but it did mean that large-scale Oeridian settlement tended to stop at the forest edge.

The plains were for men.

The green shadows were not.

---

## The Eastern Pressure

The eastern mountains and hard hills were a different matter.

There the Oeridians faced a constant and more material threat:

- **orcs**, who raided outward in bands whenever the plains seemed weak  
- **dwarves**, who held deeper stone strongholds and traded when it suited them, fought when it did not

The Oeridians never fully mastered the mountains.

Instead they learned to defend the plains against whatever came down from them.

This frontier pressure helped shape several enduring Oeridian traits:

- strong cavalry traditions
- rapid mustering by horn and fire
- distrust of mountain roads not watched by their own scouts
- respect for dwarven craft, even when the dwarves themselves were regarded as stubborn and alien

The later fortification culture of Gorundia owes much to this early mountain struggle.

---

## The Northern Riders

North of the settled plains, the Oeridians shared kin and custom with harsher riding peoples whose lives remained more fully tied to open pasture and migration.

These northern bands were not yet a separate civilization, but the distinction was already forming.

Some tribes:

- wintered farther north
- moved more widely
- lived by herding, raiding, and horse-breeding
- cared little for hillforts or fixed fields

In later centuries these northern peoples would diverge more sharply and become the ancestors of the [[Qazaq Nomads]].

At this stage, however, the divide was more one of habit than blood.

The future farmer and the future steppe rider still recognized one another as kin.

---

## The Southern Drift

Not all Oeridian tribes remained in the western plains.

Some moved south and southeast into the broader valleys beyond the mountains and foothills, especially in the centuries after the great catastrophes of the old world.

There they encountered refugee Suel communities and the emerging military society that would later become the [[Khoroûvsk Dominion]].

These eastern and southern Oeridians did not all meet the same fate.

Some became:

- allies and cavalry clients
- tributary tribes
- trade partners
- mercenary auxiliaries

Others were reduced to harsher dependence and became little better than subjects or slaves under stronger Suel-directed powers.

This early entanglement is one reason the Dominion and Gorundia would later share certain cultural features despite becoming bitter enemies.

Both inherited part of the same Oeridian stock.

---

## Before the Plow

The Oeridians of this age were not yet the settled people later generations would recognize as Gorundians.

Large-scale agriculture remained limited.

Most power still rode on horseback.

The great transformation had not yet occurred.

But the memory endured.

Later Gorundians would preserve sayings such as:

- “Our fathers rode where the wheat now grows.”
- “Before the plow, there was the saddle.”

Such sayings were not poetry alone.

They were history.

---

## Legacy

The Oeridian tribes laid the human foundations of what would one day become Gorundia.

From them came:

- the old horse traditions
- the clan loyalties later reshaped into noble houses
- the warrior ethos that would align so strongly with Heironeous
- the stubborn sense that free men of the plains should not bow easily to distant tyrants

They were not yet a kingdom.

They were the people from which a kingdom could be made.
