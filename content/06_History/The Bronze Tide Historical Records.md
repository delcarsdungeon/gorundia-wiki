## **Archivist’s Note**

These records concern a draconic entity consistently identified across sources as:

> **“The Bronze of the Storm Tide.”**

Descriptions align with a **fully draconic form**, exhibiting equal dominion in:

- air
- open sea
- submerged depths

---

No source describes a humanoid manifestation.

All credible accounts agree:

> The entity interacted with maritime activity not as predator, but as **conditional influence on outcome**.

---

## **Observed Functions (Cross-Referenced)**

Across independent sources, the following behaviors are consistent:

- Did not pursue or attack non-hostile vessels without cause
- Did not punish refusal of tithe
- Actively disrupted piracy and systemic predation
- Altered environmental outcomes surrounding marked vessels
- Exhibited selective, targeted violence when system integrity was threatened

---

## **Terminology Clarification**

> The term _“protection”_ is considered inaccurate.

More precise interpretations include:

- **Inclusion within a controlled outcome framework**
- **Reduction of catastrophic variance**
- **Environmental alignment in favor of marked vessels**

---

## **The Mark (General Consensus)**

The so-called “mark” is:

- Not visible in normal conditions
- Not reproducible through known magical or alchemical means
- Not persistent independent of circumstance

It is best understood as:

> **Recognition by the entity, not a symbol applied by it**

Forgery attempts universally resulted in catastrophic failure (see appended incidents).

---

## **Behavioral Pattern**

The entity demonstrated three distinct modes of interaction:

1. **Non-Intervention**
    - Unmarked vessels experienced natural conditions without alteration
2. **Conditional Alignment**
    - Marked vessels experienced statistically reduced catastrophic outcomes
3. **Corrective Action**
    - Applied in cases of piracy, systemic disruption, or attempted exploitation
    - Characterized by overwhelming, targeted lethality

---

## **Outlier Events**

A limited number of accounts describe:

- Intervention on behalf of vessels unable to provide tithe
- Subsequent long-term behavioral alignment of beneficiaries

Interpretations vary:

- anomaly
- deliberate investment
- misclassification of subject

---

## **Geographic Correlation**

Multiple records place sightings or influence zones near:

- major trade routes of former Elenlindë
- open waters surrounding [[Draconis Isle]]

Relationship to the Isle remains:

> **Undetermined**

---

## **Preliminary Conclusion**

The Bronze of the Storm Tide functioned not as:

- deity
- guardian
- predator

But as:

> **A stabilizing system applied to maritime risk**

---

Failure to correctly interpret this role has historically resulted in:

- total vessel loss
- unexplained environmental anomalies
- or targeted elimination of offending parties

## **The Bronze of the Storm Tide**

_As preserved in the leaf-archives of the Verdant Invocation, where sea-wind has worn the edges smooth and even memory tastes faintly of salt._

---

There was a time—before the present tide, before men learned to name their losses and call them fate—when the sea was not lawless.

It was not gentle.

It was not cruel.

It was _kept_.

Not by crown, nor god, nor spell alone, but by a presence that moved between wave and storm as though both were merely different expressions of the same breath.

The old records do not soften this truth:

She was no spirit.

No omen.

No fleeting thing of mist and metaphor.

She was **dragon**.

---

**Bronze, as the storm-tide is bronze at dusk—  
Not gold, not red, but something older,  
Something that remembers the first thunder.**

---

She did not stand upon the sea.

She moved through it as it was meant to be moved through.

- Her wings broke the sky    
- Her body cut the waves    
- Her talons touched water as easily as stone    

Where she passed, the ocean did not yield.

It **aligned**.

---

For the sea does not bow.

It does not kneel.

It does not obey.

---

It recognizes.

---

And it recognized her.

---

Storms bent—not in fear, but in acknowledgment.

Lightning coiled along her scales and found no purchase.

The wind shifted around her passage, not diverted, but _accounted for_.

And the great currents, those slow and patient rivers beneath the waves, held their courses when she was near.

---

Where she went, the sea remembered itself.

---

Ships that entered those waters were not hunted.

They were not spared.

They were **weighed**.

---

A hull is a promise.

A crew is a covenant.

A voyage is a line drawn across the living skin of the world.

She read these things not with eye alone, but with something older—something that tasted intent as the deep tastes pressure.

---

If a vessel held true—

If its oaths were kept, its hands honest, its weight carried as it should be—

Then the storm would pass it by.

The waves would rise, but not break.

The dark things beneath would turn aside, as predators do when the hunt is not theirs to claim.

---

If not—

Nothing changed.

---

The storm still came.

The sea still rose.

The deep still reached upward with its cold and patient hands.

---

And she did nothing.

---

This was the law.

Not written.

Not spoken.

But known.

---

For a long age—long enough for even the elder courts of Elenlindë to mistake pattern for permanence —the tide held to this measure.

Trade flowed.

Routes stabilized.

Men called it fortune.

Elves called it balance.

Neither named her.

---

As is our way when we grow comfortable.

---

But there came a crossing—

There is always such a crossing—

That did not belong to the tide.

---

Its hull bore weight that did not settle.

Its wake did not follow the current.

Its oaths rang hollow, as though spoken into a place that did not answer.

---

And still—

She marked it.

---

Whether by error, or will, or a judgment no longer understood, the measure faltered.

Not shattered.

Not broken.

But… _misapplied_.

---

And beneath the sea—

In the places where even dragons do not swim—

Something listened.

---

There are depths where water is not water.

Where the dark does not lift.

Where even memory, once sunk, does not return to the surface.

---

In such places, there are no currents.

No tides.

No storms.

---

Only stillness.

---

And within that stillness—

Things that do not demand.

Do not rise.

Do not claim.

---

They **offer**.

---

She descended.

---

Not as prey.

Not as conqueror.

But as something that had always understood systems… and now sought one deeper than the tide itself.

---

What passed there is not recorded.

Not clearly.

Not consistently.

---

Some say she drank.

Some say she listened.

Some say she answered.

---

All agree on this:

She rose again.

---

And the sea did not know her.

---

Not as it had.

---

The waves still moved.

The storms still gathered.

The deep still breathed in its slow and terrible way.

---

But where once there had been certainty—

There was now… hesitation.

---

Lightning struck her.

Not in defiance—

But without recognition.

---

The currents shifted beneath her passage, not aligning, but slipping.

The great unseen weights of the ocean no longer balanced cleanly in her presence.

---

The measure remained.

But it was no longer singular.

---

Where once there had been judgment—

There was now… variance.

---

Ships survived that should not have.

Others were taken without cause.

Storms came too early.

Or too late.

Or not at all.

---

The tide had not lost its power.

---

It had lost its **voice**.

---

We do not say she fell.

That is the language of men, who require endings.

---

We do not say she was corrupted.

That is the language of mages, who require causes.

---

We say only this:

---

**The Bronze of the Storm Tide descended where no current flows,  
and returned bearing something the sea does not remember how to answer.**

---

And even now—

When the storm hesitates before it breaks…

When the waves rise without certainty…

When the deep feels… _misaligned_—

---

There are those who claim to see her.

---

Not as a woman.

Not as a shadow.

---

But as she has always been—

---

A vast shape of bronze and storm,

Moving between sky and sea,

As though listening—

---

For a voice

That once answered her

Without question.


---

## **Fragment — The Ledger of Unwarranted Conclusions**

_Attributed to Magister Halveth Ruun, Alchemist of Galan Lenora (status: missing, presumed taken by the sea, dated 4 Sunsebb, 987)_

---

I record this not as confession—  
though it will be taken as such—  
but as correction.

I was wrong.

Not in method.

Not in theory.

In premise.

---

I believed the phenomenon to be transferable.  The mark of the storm-tide.

Replicable.

Abstractable from the source.

---

I had observed the effect:

- reduced variance in storm interaction
- statistical collapse of catastrophic outcomes
- behavioral alteration in proximate natural systems

These are _measurable things_.

Therefore, I concluded:

> it could be reproduced.

---

I was not the first.

I was simply the most thorough.

---

The compound held.

The sigil stabilized.

The test hull retained structural integrity under simulated surge conditions beyond expected thresholds.

Even divination returned—

_consistency_.

---

I allowed myself a moment of professional satisfaction.

That was my final error.

---

She did not come during the experiment.

Nor during the first application.

Nor even when the vessel departed harbor under false conditions.

---

She came to my house.

---

There was no storm.

That is important.

There was no warning, no shift in air, no heralding phenomenon.

---

There was simply—

weight.

---

The roof failed first.

Not shattered.

Not struck.

It… yielded.

As though it had been asked, quietly, to stop existing as a roof.

---

Then the bow of the vessel—my vessel, my work, my proof—  
rested where my study had been.

---

No fire.

No collapse beyond the immediate.

Just… presence.

---

She followed it.

---

I had read descriptions.

All insufficient.

All wrong in the same way a diagram of a storm is wrong.

---

She did not enter.

She did not lower herself.

She did not need to.

---

Her head filled the space where my ceiling had been.

Bronze—not bright, not gleaming—  
but darkened, like metal that has known lightning and kept the memory.

---

Her eyes did not burn.

They _weighed_.

---

I prepared speech.

Explanations.

Proofs.

I had, in that moment, a dozen arguments for why my work did not threaten her function.

---

I did not speak them.

---

Because I understood—too late—that she was not there to debate.

---

She looked at the hull.

---

Not at the sigil.

Not at the compound.

At the _thing itself_.

---

There was a pause.

Not long.

Not dramatic.

Just enough for a conclusion to be reached.

---

Then she looked at me.

---

There are no adequate words for that moment.

Not fear.

Not awe.

---

Recognition.

---

Complete.

Immediate.

Unavoidable.

---

I understood, with a clarity I have never known before or since:

> I had not created a mark.  
> I had created a lie  
> and asked the world to believe it.

---

She said nothing.

---

Not because she could not.

But because there was nothing to clarify.

---

Her talon touched the hull.

Once.

Lightly.

---

The sound it made—

I have tried to describe it.

It was not impact.

It was not fracture.

---

It was _revision_.

---

The bow of the ship—

my ship—

ceased to be.

---

Not destroyed.

Not broken.

---

Removed.

---

The space it occupied did not collapse.

It simply… no longer contained it.

---

My notes burned after.

Not by flame.

By… failure.

Ink lost cohesion.

Paper forgot its structure.

Meaning itself degraded.

---

I have attempted to reconstruct what I can.

This document is incomplete.

It will remain so.

---

I do not believe she came to punish me.

---

I believe—

with a certainty I cannot justify—

that she came to correct the error.

---

And I was part of it.

---

If this is found, let it stand as final amendment:

---

> The mark is not placed upon the vessel.  
> The vessel is placed within the mark.

---

I will not continue this work.

---

I do not believe I am permitted to.

---

There is a sound now, beneath the floor.

Not water.

Not movement.

---

## **Fragment — The First Net of Hadrik Fen**

_Recorded in the coastal rolls of Saltreach, copied thrice, amended once by an irritated clerk_

---

In the years before the routes were fully kept, when the smaller boats still trusted more to weather than to coin, there lived a fisherman called Hadrik Fen.

He owned no vessel worth naming.

A narrow craft.  
Warped ribs.  
A net mended more often than it was cast.

---

On the morning in question, the tide was wrong.

Not visibly.

Not to the untrained.

But those who lived by it would later say:

> “The water felt… hurried.”

---

Hadrik had nothing to offer.

No coffer.

No tithe.

No coin he could spare without choosing hunger instead.

---

He went out anyway.

As poor men do.

---

By midday, the horizon had darkened.

Not in the slow gathering way of weather, but in a sudden closing—as though something had decided the day was finished.

---

The wind turned.

Then turned again.

Then ceased to follow any pattern known to those who survived by such knowledge.

---

Other boats began to pull back.

Some made it.

Some did not.

---

Hadrik did not turn.

Later, when asked why, he would say:

> “I didn’t think I’d make shore either way.”

---

The storm rose.

Not violent at first.

Not dramatic.

Just… _certain_.

---

And then—

She was there.

---

Not descending.

Not arriving.

---

The water lifted—

and did not fall away.

---

She rose from it so smoothly it seemed she had always been there,

only now… _seen_.

---

No surge.

No crash.

---

Just the slow reveal of bronze through dark water,

as though the sea itself were remembering her shape.

---

When she cleared the surface, the tide clung to her—

long strands of black-green water slipping from scale and talon,

falling in heavy, deliberate drops.

---

Not shedding.

Not shaken free.

---

Released.

---

And when the last of it fell,

she was simply—

---

Present.
---

He would later struggle to describe her.

Not because he lacked words.

But because all the words he had felt too small, and he knew it.

---

He said only this:

> “She was bigger than the storm,  
> and the storm knew it.”

---

She did not look at him first.

---

She looked at the water.

---

A long moment.

A stillness within motion.

---

Then her head turned, and her gaze fell on him.

---

Hadrik Fen, who had nothing.

Who had offered nothing.

Who had no place in any ledger worth keeping.

---

He did not speak.

He did not plead.

He did not pray.

---

He held the net.

---

And waited.

---

What passed in that moment is not recorded the same way twice.

---

Some say she turned away.

Some say she lingered.

Some say the storm bent around him as it had for greater vessels.

---

But all accounts agree on what followed.

---

The sea… changed.

---

Not calmed.

Not stilled.

---

_Directed._

---

The currents shifted beneath his boat—not violently, but with purpose.

The wind did not cease, but it ceased to matter.

And the net in his hands grew heavy.

---

Too heavy.

---

He hauled it once.

Twice.

A third time, bracing his feet against wood that should not have held him.

---

The net broke the surface—

---

Silver.

---

Not scattered.

Not fleeing.

---

Gathered.

---

A school so dense it moved like a single creature, turning as one, pressing against itself as though guided.

---

He filled his hold.

Then filled it again.

Then tied the net off because there was no more room for what it contained.

---

Only then did she move.

---

She did not take flight.

She did not vanish.

---

She turned.

---

And the sea followed.

---

Hadrik would later say:

> “I didn’t steer after that.”

---

The storm did not end.

It continued.

Behind him.

Around him.

Never _through_ him.

---

He reached shore at dusk.

---

No other small boats returned that day.

---

In the years that followed, Hadrik Fen prospered.

Not wildly.

Not beyond reason.

---

But steadily.

---

He paid, when he could.

Then when he should.

Then when he no longer needed to think about it.

---

He taught others:

- how to read the water
    
- how to keep honest weight
    
- how to speak plainly of what they carried
    

---

And always—

always—

to offer what was due before it was asked.

---

When asked why she had spared him, he never claimed favor.

Never claimed blessing.

---

He would only say:

> “I think… she didn’t count me yet.”

## **Fragment — Testimony of Rell “Three-Fingers” Vorc**

_Taken at Black Harbor Inquest, recorded by order of the reeve, amended twice for coherence_

---

You want it straight?

You won’t get it straight.

But I’ll tell it.

---

We had four ships.

Not raiders—don’t write that.

We were… organized.

We knew the routes.  
Knew when the little boats ran light, when the big ones ran heavy.

We took from both.

---

We’d heard the stories.

Everyone had.

---

We didn’t believe them.

---

First mistake.

---

Sea was calm that day.

Too calm, maybe, if you’re the sort that listens for that kind of thing.

We weren’t.

---

We took the first prize clean.

Second too.

Crew tossed over, cargo split, nothing messy.

Good work.

---

Then the water—

---

I don’t know how to say this right.

It didn’t _move_.

It changed its mind.

---

The third ship—our left flank—

just… stopped.

---

No impact.

No warning.

---

The hull opened from beneath like it had been unmade.

---

Not smashed.

Not torn.

---

Opened.

---

Men went down with it before they had time to scream.

The ship folded in on itself like rotten wood, except it wasn’t rotten.

I’d seen that hull. Solid.

---

Gone in seconds.

Just… gone.

---

We thought it was reef.

Hidden rock.

Bad chart.

---

Second mistake.

---

Then she came up.

---

Not breaking the surface.

Not bursting through.

---

The water rose around her—

and she was there.

---

Head and shoulders first, bronze slick with black water that ran from her like something reluctant to let go.

---

And the sky—

---

The sky noticed.

---

Lightning didn’t fall.

It _answered_.

---

She turned—

slow, like she had all the time in the world—

and the second ship got it.

---

Full.

---

Mast to keel.

---

There wasn’t fire.

Not like you’d think.

There was light—

and then there was less ship than there had been a moment before.

---

Men were still burning when they hit the water.

---

We broke formation.

Didn’t even call it.

Just… broke.

---

Tried to scatter.

---

Third mistake.

---

She waited.

---

That’s what I remember most.

Not the lightning.

Not the first ship going under.

---

The waiting.

---

She sank back beneath the water.

Not gone.

Just… not visible.

---

And for a heartbeat—

two—

three—

---

Nothing.

---

Then the sea—

---

I’ve seen whales breach.

Big ones.

You feel it in your bones when they come up.

---

This—

---

This made that feel small.

---

She came out of the water like the ocean had decided to throw her.

---

Spray went higher than the mast.

The sound—

I don’t have a word for that sound.

---

She hit the third ship mid-air.

---

Not landed.

Hit.

---

Claws through deck.

Wings snapping rigging like thread.

Weight alone broke her spine.

---

The ship didn’t sink right away.

It… sagged.

Then went.

---

That left us.

---

Fourth ship.

Mine.

---

No.

Don’t write that.

Not mine.

I was crew.

Just crew.

---

She came down on us quiet.

---

No lightning.

No crashing.

---

Just weight.

---

Deck dipped under her claws like it was soft.

Men froze.

I froze.

---

She moved slow.

That’s the worst of it.

---

Not a frenzy.

Not rage.

---

Selection.

---

She took the first man with her teeth.

Didn’t shake him.

Didn’t tear.

Just—

removed him.

---

Second with a claw.

Lifted him off the deck like he weighed nothing.

Dropped what was left.

---

Some tried to fight.

I remember that.

Brave bastards.

---

Didn’t matter.

---

She walked the deck like she was counting.

---

One.

Two.

Three.

---

Men died quiet.

Or screaming.

Didn’t seem to matter to her.

---

She left the captain for last.

---

Of course she did.

---

He tried to run.

Slipped.

Fell.

Crawled.

---

She pinned him.

One talon through the coat, into the wood beneath.

Held him there while he screamed.

---

And she—

---

She looked at him.

---

Longer than she had at any of us.

---

I thought—

I thought maybe she’d speak.

---

She didn’t.

---

She took him in her jaws.

Careful.

Not crushing.

---

And then—

---

Back into the water.

---

Not diving.

Not splashing.

---

Just… gone.

---

With him still screaming.

---

We drifted after that.

No storm.

No wind.

---

Just the sound of water.

---
