# Qazaq Nomads

> “There are only two kinds of men.  
> Those who ride free, and those who serve someone.”  
> — traditional Qazaq saying

North of the Oeridian plains lived the riding peoples who would later become known collectively as the **Qazaq Nomads**.

Originally they were little different from the Oeridian tribes who occupied the western plains. The same languages were spoken, the same horses were bred, and the same clan structures governed their lives.

Over time, however, their way of life hardened into something far more extreme.

The northern riders abandoned permanent settlement almost entirely.

They became a people of the saddle.

---

## Children of the Steppe

The lands north of Gorundia were vast grazing plains where no permanent frontier could easily be drawn.

Winter storms were brutal.

Summer grass was rich but fleeting.

Only a highly mobile society could survive there.

The Qazaq adapted by building their entire culture around:

- mounted warfare  
- large migratory herds  
- mobile encampments  
- clan loyalty over territory  

A Qazaq clan might ride hundreds of miles between seasonal camps.

Their homes were wagons and felt tents.

Their wealth walked on four legs.

A man’s true status was measured in horses and followers.

---

## The Free Riders

Unlike their cousins in the south, the Qazaq rejected nearly every form of centralized rule.

No king governed them.

No temple hierarchy commanded them.

Leadership among the Qazaq was temporary and personal.

War leaders rose through:

- battlefield success  
- generosity with plunder  
- personal charisma  
- the loyalty of their riders  

A weak leader simply lost followers.

The Qazaq proudly claimed that **no man ruled them unless he could ride beside them in war**.

This belief would become the core of their identity.

To this day the Qazaq proclaim:

> “We are the only free men in the world.”

---

## A People of Many Bloods

The Qazaq were never a single tribe.

They became a **fusion of many displaced peoples**.

Among their ancestors were:

- northern Oeridian riders  
- escaped slaves from Dominion territories  
- raiding orc clans driven from the mountains  
- half-orcs born along the frontier  
- deserters, outlaws, and refugees from countless wars  

On the open steppe survival mattered more than ancestry.

Anyone who could ride, fight, and live by the clan’s law could be accepted.

Over generations this mixture produced a population that was heavily **half-orc in blood and appearance**, though their cultural roots remained strongly Oeridian.

This gave the Qazaq their distinctive look:

broad-shouldered riders, weathered by wind and sun, often bearing the tusks or heavy features of orcish ancestry.

Among themselves this was a badge of pride.

Strength was proof of freedom.

---

## Law of the Saddle

Despite their reputation as raiders, the Qazaq lived by a strict unwritten code.

Among the steppe clans several rules were universally recognized:

- betrayal of one’s clan was punishable by death  
- hospitality to travelers was sacred  
- disputes between riders were settled in open challenge  
- stolen horses must be avenged  

This harsh but consistent law allowed the clans to maintain order despite lacking permanent governments.

The greatest crime among the Qazaq was not murder or theft.

It was **submission**.

A man who willingly served a tyrant was pitied or despised.

---

## Relations with the South

The relationship between the Qazaq and the southern plains peoples was complicated.

To the early Oeridian tribes, the Qazaq were:

- cousins
- trading partners
- occasional raiders

Later, when the kingdoms of Gorundia and the Dominion arose, the Qazaq remained fiercely independent.

Both states attempted to control the steppe.

Both failed.

Instead they relied on diplomacy, tribute, and mercenary service to manage relations with the riders.

Qazaq horsemen frequently appeared as:

- scouts
- mercenaries
- caravan guards
- raiding allies

But they never accepted permanent rule.

Empires could tax cities.

They could not tax the wind.

---

## The Steppe Myth

Among the Qazaq themselves a powerful origin myth developed.

They claimed their ancestors were the **first humans to reject the chains of kings**.

According to their stories, the ancestors of the Qazaq once lived under tyrants who tried to command their lives.

Rather than submit, the riders fled into the endless plains.

There they swore never again to bow to a throne.

Whether true or not, this belief shaped their entire worldview.

The Qazaq see themselves not merely as a people, but as a **living defiance of tyranny**.

---

## Legacy

Though they never formed a kingdom of their own, the Qazaq influenced every civilization around them.

Their legacy includes:

- the finest light cavalry traditions in the region  
- trade routes across the northern steppe  
- mercenary riders who served in countless wars  
- a culture of fierce independence that continues to inspire frontier peoples  

Even powerful states like Gorundia and the Dominion treat the Qazaq with caution.

For a people who own no cities and build few walls, the Qazaq have proven remarkably difficult to conquer.

Freedom, once learned in the saddle, is not easily taken away.

# Major Qazaq Clans

### **Clan Karzug**

_“The Iron Riders”_

Named after a legendary half-orc war chief who supposedly broke the spine of a Dominion cavalry commander with his bare hands.

Traits:

- massive horse herds    
- elite heavy lancers    
- most physically imposing riders    

Reputation: brutal but honorable.

Their war banner is a **black horse skull on red felt**.

---

### **Clan Temir-Khan**

_“The Steel Horde”_

The most disciplined of the Qazaq clans.

They pride themselves on:

- tight cavalry formations    
- battlefield tactics    
- mercenary contracts with foreign powers    

Many Temir-Khan riders serve as **professional steppe mercenaries**.

They say:

> “A free man can still sell his sword.”

Just never his soul.

---

### **Clan Borcha**

_“The Storm Riders”_

Fastest horses on the steppe.

Known for:

- lightning raids    
- mounted archery    
- disappearing into the plains before retaliation    

Dominion caravans absolutely hate them.

Their riders decorate saddles with **hawk feathers**.

---

### **Clan Yargut**

_“The Broken Chain Clan”_

Descended largely from:

- escaped Dominion slaves    
- half-orc laborers    
- runaway soldiers    

They are the **most aggressively anti-Dominion** clan.

Their clan symbol is a **broken manacle**.

If a Dominion officer is captured by Yargut riders, he usually dies very slowly.

---

### **Clan Shulgatai**

_“The Wolf Blood Clan”_

The most visibly half-orc clan.

Many riders have:

- tusks    
- heavy brows    
- massive frames    

They claim descent from an orc warband that abandoned the mountains and took the saddle.

Other clans respect them for sheer battlefield ferocity.

Their banner shows a **white wolf on black felt**.

---

# The High Assembly of the Steppe

Once every few years the clans gather at a massive meeting called the:

**Kurultai of the Free Riders**

Here they decide things like:

- major wars    
- alliances with kingdoms    
- selection of a temporary **War Khan**    

Important rule:

The War Khan commands **only during the campaign**.

Afterward he goes back to being just another rider.

This is why the Qazaq remain impossible to permanently unify.

They _refuse_ to stay unified.

---

# Classic Qazaq Saying

> “Cities have kings.  
> Mountains have tyrants.
> 
> The steppe has riders.”

---

## **Arslan the Unbridled**

_Hero of the Steppe, Bandit of the Plains, Terror of the Frontier_

> “A horse may accept a bit.  
> A man should not.”  
> — attributed to Arslan

Among the Qazaq nomads there are countless songs about a rider known as **Arslan the Unbridled**.

To the Qazaq he is the greatest hero of the steppe.  
To the Dominion he is a savage outlaw.  
To the Gorundians he is something in between — a dangerous man who nonetheless fought tyrants.

No historian agrees on exactly when he lived, but most place him roughly **four centuries before the founding of Gorundia**, during the early centuries of steppe migration.

---

## The Rider Without a Clan

According to the oldest songs, Arslan was born to no tribe.

Some claim he was:

- the son of an Oeridian rider and an orc raider
    
- a runaway slave from a Dominion caravan
    
- the bastard of a steppe warlord and a captive woman
    

What all versions agree on is that he **refused to serve any chief**.

He rode alone for years, gathering followers slowly — men who had quarreled with their clans, escaped slaves, deserters, and wandering fighters.

His band became known as the **Free Riders**.

---

## The Burning of Three Banners

The story that made Arslan famous occurred when a Dominion war column attempted to establish authority over several steppe clans.

The Dominion commander raised three banners:

- the imperial banner of Kesh
    
- the war-banner of Hextor
    
- the banner of the governing house
    

According to Qazaq tradition, Arslan rode into the Dominion camp at night with only a handful of companions.

They cut down the banner poles and burned the standards.

When the commander demanded submission the next morning, Arslan reportedly laughed and said:

> “Your banners burn well.  
> Your empire will burn the same.”

The Dominion army eventually drove the riders off, but the story spread across the steppe.

From that moment Arslan became a symbol of defiance.

---

## The Duel at the Black Ford

Another famous story claims that Arslan once fought a Dominion knight-champion in single combat at a river crossing known as **the Black Ford**.

The knight fought in heavy armor.

Arslan wore only leather and furs.

The duel ended when Arslan dragged the armored knight from his saddle and drowned him in the river.

Qazaq riders love this story.

Dominion historians insist it is exaggerated.

Gorundian chronicles record it as **“possibly true.”**

---

## Death and Disappearance

Like many steppe heroes, Arslan’s death is uncertain.

Different traditions claim he:

- died fighting a Dominion army
    
- vanished into the northern steppe
    
- rode west into the forests and never returned
    

The most popular Qazaq belief is simpler.

They say Arslan **never died**.

He simply rode farther than any man had ridden before.

---

## Cultural Legacy

Arslan’s legend plays a different role in each culture.

### Among the Qazaq

He is the **ideal free rider**.

A man who bows to no chief, no priest, and no king.

Young riders still swear oaths “by the saddle of Arslan.”

---

### In Gorundia

Stories of Arslan are told with caution.

Some knights admire his defiance of tyranny.

Others see him as a warning about the dangers of uncontrolled steppe power.

Either way, he remains a figure of fascination.

---

### In the Dominion

Dominion historians portray Arslan as proof of what happens when order collapses.

Their chronicles describe him as:

> “A bandit chief whose rebellion illustrates the necessity of firm rule.”

Naturally, Qazaq storytellers find this interpretation hilarious.

---

## The Steppe Saying

Among the riders one phrase survives from the oldest songs:

> “Ride far enough and no king can reach you.”

They claim Arslan said it first.
