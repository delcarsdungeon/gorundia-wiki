# 060 — Settlement of Gorundia

> “Our fathers rode where the wheat now grows.”  
> — Old Gorundian saying

The **Settlement of Gorundia** marks the transition from a loose network of **Oeridian tribal lands** into the earliest permanent societies of the Gorundian plains.  

For centuries the region between the western forests and the eastern mountains had been inhabited by **pastoral horse-clans**, moving seasonally with their herds and defending their territory from raiders, monsters, and rival tribes.

But slowly — almost unnoticed at first — the land began to change.

The saddle gave way to the plow.

---

## The Land Before Settlement

Prior to permanent settlement the region that would become Gorundia existed as a **three-frontier ecosystem**.

**West — The Deep Forests**  
The vast forests belonged to the **elves** and their druidic allies.  
Human tribes learned early that wandering too far beneath those canopies invited silent warnings from elven wardens.

**East — The Mountains**  
The highlands were dominated by **dwarven holds** and numerous **orc tribes**.  
Orc raiders periodically descended into the plains, forcing the human tribes to develop strong cavalry traditions.

**North and East — The Steppe**  
The open grasslands were the domain of the **Qazaq nomads**, horse-lords who refused permanent settlement and maintained a fiercely independent culture.

**The Plains — Oeridian Territory**  
Between these powers stretched wide fertile plains where the **Oeridian tribes** grazed horses and cattle.

For centuries this balance remained relatively stable.

---

## The Agricultural Shift

The transformation began when several tribes began experimenting with **large-scale grain cultivation**.

Historians attribute this development to several converging influences:

- knowledge brought by **Suloise refugees**
- trade with **dwarven mountain holds**
- gradual population pressure among the tribes

Permanent fields meant **permanent settlements**.

Where once only seasonal camps existed, villages began to appear.

Soon after came:

- wooden palisades
- storage granaries
- permanent shrines
- fortified hilltop settlements

These early towns formed the nucleus of what would later become Gorundian cities.

---

## Tribal Consolidation

Agriculture created a new problem.

Fields and irrigation systems required **stable borders and protection**.

This forced the Oeridian clans to begin forming **larger confederations**.

Several powerful tribal groups emerged across the plains.

Notable among them were early versions of the clans remembered today:

- **Clan Ryzan**
- **Clan Belovar**
- **Clan Torvach**
- **Clan Vaskor**
- **Clan Drovan**

These alliances marked the beginning of **proto-Gorundian identity**.

The clans still valued freedom and mobility, but their lives were now tied to land that could not move with them.

---

## The Steppe Divide

Not all tribes accepted this change.

Many riders rejected the idea of permanent settlement entirely.

These groups rode eastward and continued the traditional nomadic life, eventually becoming the culture now known as the **Qazaq**.

To this day, Qazaq riders mock the settlers of Gorundia with a common saying:

> “You traded the wind for fences.”

The settlers, in turn, considered the nomads relics of an older age.

---

## Influence of the Dwarves

Trade with the dwarven holds of the eastern mountains accelerated the development of settlement.

Dwarves supplied the plains tribes with:

- iron plowshares
- mining tools
- stoneworking knowledge
- early fortification methods

In exchange they received:

- grain
- livestock
- horse trade
- access to lowland trade routes

These relationships helped transform fragile tribal villages into **defensible towns**.

---

## The First Roads

As settlements expanded, trade between them increased.

Paths between seasonal camps gradually hardened into **permanent roads**.

These routes allowed:

- regular trade caravans
- communication between tribal confederations
- rapid military response to orc raids

Many of Gorundia’s modern highways still follow the same ancient routes.

---

## Cultural Memory

Even after permanent settlement became dominant, Gorundians never fully forgot their origins.

Old sayings still reflect the transition from nomad to farmer:

> “Before the plow, there was the saddle.”

> “A Gorundian owns land — but the wind still owns his heart.”

These traditions preserved the cultural memory of a time when the plains were ruled by riders rather than kings.

---

## Historical Significance

The **Settlement of Gorundia** represents the crucial turning point between tribal society and statehood.

Once agriculture and permanent towns took root, the next steps became inevitable:

- centralized leadership
- standing armies
- taxation and administration
- the rise of noble houses

Within a few generations, the tribal confederations of the plains would produce the first true Gorundian state.

That moment is remembered as the **[[Founding of Gorundia]]**.
