# Blood and Sand — Late Night

The tavern had gone to embers and murmurs.

A single lantern swung slowly over the bar.  
The kids were asleep in a heap upstairs.  
Somewhere in the back, a keg ticked as it cooled.

Gravel sat with his boots stretched out, one heel hooked on a rung that had seen better decades.

His knuckles were scraped.  
His shirt was open.  
He looked like a man who’d spent all day carrying other people’s problems.

“She didn’t show,” he said.

Grenda didn’t look up from the rag she was dragging over the table.

“I noticed.”

“She always shows.”

“She didn’t.”

He exhaled through his nose.

“Where?”

Grenda set the rag down.

“East Gate.”

He frowned.

“That’s a long walk.”

“She didn’t stop there.”

He looked up at that.

“She went past it. All the way out to the halfling farms. Borrowed a handcart. Hauled back flour, onions, a sack of barley, whatever they’d spare.”

Gravel’s jaw tightened.

“By herself.”

“By herself.”

“She could’ve said something.”

“She didn’t want fuss.”

“That ain’t fuss,” he snapped. “That’s sense. I’d have gotten her a horse. Proper cart. Two hands to walk with her. Nobody’d so much as breathe wrong in her direction.”

Grenda leaned back against the bar, broad arms folded.

“She don’t want protection like that.”

“She needs it.”

“She doesn’t think so.”

Gravel’s fingers drummed against the table.

“She ain’t big. She ain’t mean. She’s got them robes and that tin sun thing. That’s it.”

“She’s got more than that.”

He glanced up.

“She walked into Hin country,” Grenda said. “East of the walls. Knocked on doors. Didn’t beg. Didn’t bow. Just said, *‘The Dregs are hungry.’*”

Gravel stared at the floor.

“They gave?” he muttered.

“They filled the cart.”

Silence.

“She didn’t ask me,” he said again, but softer now.

“No,” Grenda agreed. “She don’t ask. Not for coin. Not for guards. Not for anything.”

“That’s damn foolish.”

“That’s why they trust her.”

He grunted at that, unhappy.

“She’d have taken a horse if I’d put it under her,” he insisted.

Grenda snorted.

“No. She’d have walked beside it so it didn’t feel overworked.”

He barked a laugh despite himself.

“Stubborn little sun-priest.”

“Mm.”

He rubbed a hand over his face.

“Next time she goes through East Gate,” he muttered, “I’m putting two lads shadowing her whether she likes it or not.”

Grenda picked up her mug.

“You can try.”

He shot her a look.

“She’ll notice,” Grenda said. “And she’ll walk twice as far just to prove she don’t need ’em.”

Gravel huffed.

“Fine,” he said. “Then I’ll make sure East Gate’s quiet that day.”

Grenda clinked her mug against his.

“That,” she said, “she’ll never know.”

They drank to that—

to stubborn faith,  
to long roads east of the walls,  
and to protecting someone who’d never ask for it.
