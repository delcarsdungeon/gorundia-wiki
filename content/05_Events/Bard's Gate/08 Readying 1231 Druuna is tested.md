# Blood and Sand — Late Afternoon

The **Blood and Sand** was alive in its usual way—laughter like falling masonry, tankards hitting wood hard enough to mean something, smoke coiling under the rafters. The place smelled of roasted meat, sweat, and ambition.

**[[Gravel]]** occupied the corner booth.

Back to stone.  
View of the door.

No one ever said it was the office.

Everyone knew it was.

Across from him stood a gnome with ink-darkened fingertips and a ledger too big for his frame.

“Write it like this,” Gravel said, tapping the parchment with two thick fingers.  
“By authority vested in the reeve and witnessed by sworn men of the ward, patrol adjustments are hereby enacted.”

The gnome nodded quickly.

“Yes, Master Gravel. Shall I include the levy amendment—”

The tavern door opened.

It wasn’t dramatic.

It didn’t need to be.

Conversations shifted. Not silenced—just recalibrated.

A tall figure stood in the doorway.

Plate armor, dull from travel, not polished for applause. Scratches. Repaired straps. A sunburst worked subtly into the metal—not ornament, but oath.

His voice carried without effort.

“I am seeking the one called **Druuna**.”

Gravel didn’t look up immediately.

He finished the sentence.

“—and make sure Hin Hideaway’s stamp is on it. If it doesn’t look official, it isn’t.”

Then he lifted his eyes.

Frowned.

Nodded toward **[[Crush]]**.

Crush—wide as a wagon and just as cheerful—shifted one thick thumb toward Gravel’s booth.

Gravel turned back to the gnome.

“Pellin,” he said smoothly, “we’ll finish later.”

**[[Pellin Tumblewick]]** blinked at the armored silhouette, swallowed, and gathered parchment with impressive speed.

“Yes. Entirely lawful. Completely standard. We’ll—yes.”

He vanished.

The paladin crossed the room.

He didn’t loom.

He didn’t swagger.

He simply advanced.

Gravel gestured lazily to the empty bench across from him.

“Boss’s office.”

The paladin tilted his head.

Looked at the booth.

Realized.

Gravel meant this booth.

He did not sit.

He remained standing.

That’s when Gravel felt it.

He’d stared down **[[Orin Kharne]]**.  
He’d stood beside **[[Beringer]]** in rooms where people decided wars between courses of dinner.  
He’d grown up in alleys where hesitation got teeth broken.

This felt different.

This man didn’t feel large.

He felt **anchored**.

Like if the building collapsed, he’d still be there when the dust settled.

Gravel didn’t know the intricacies of Pelor’s vows. Didn’t recognize the faint etchings hidden in the steel.

But he knew armor.

This wasn’t ceremony.

This had **history**.

The paladin inclined his head politely.

“I am **Sir Mathren Solgard**, Knight of the Dawnfather.”

His voice was calm. Not warm. Not cold.

“I’m here on business.”

“Business is good,” Gravel replied easily.

“I seek a woman preaching Pelor’s mercy in these gutters.”

There it was.

**Druuna.**

Gravel’s jaw tightened a hair.

He leaned back, smile sliding into place like a well-used blade.

“Yeah. We know Druuna.”

Casual. Almost bored.

“Dunno where she is right now. She does her—”

He made a small circling gesture.

“—thing all over the Dregs.”

A pause.

Thinking.

If this armored sunrise was going to meet her, it would be here.

On Gravel’s ground.

Where Crush stood near the door and three half-blood legbreakers were already pretending not to pay attention.

Gravel’s smile warmed.

“You can catch her here. Every morning. Just before sunrise.”

He gave the armor a slow once-over.

“We don’t normally open for strangers.”

A beat.

“But since you’re both sun-kissed, we’ll make an exception.”

A faint twitch at the corner of the paladin’s mouth. Not quite a smile.

“Can I get you a drink while you’re here?” Gravel added lightly.  
“Ale’s strong. Water’s mostly honest.”

“I do not drink while on duty.”

“Suit yourself.”

The paladin’s gaze moved through the tavern.

Exits.  
Sightlines.  
Faces.

Crush. The legbreakers. Back to Gravel.

“You are protective of her.”

Not accusation.

Observation.

Gravel didn’t blink.

“She’s ours.”

Simple. Final.

The paladin studied him a moment longer.

“As she should be.”

That landed.

No threat. No superiority.

Just agreement.

The paladin inclined his head once.

“Before sunrise.”

He turned and left.

Noise returned. Dice clattered. Someone laughed too loudly to prove they hadn’t been listening.

Crush leaned down.

“Problem?”

Gravel stared at the door a moment longer.

“…Don’t know yet.”

He rubbed his jaw thoughtfully.

“That wasn’t a man coming to break her.”

A slow exhale.

“That was a man coming to **weigh** her.”

He glanced at the half-blood muscle stationed nearby.

“Make sure we’re ready anyway.”

Because inevitability was one thing.

But the **Blood and Sand** had survived worse than sunrise.

# Blood and Sand — Before Sunrise

The tavern was open.

That alone was strange.

Lanterns burned low and golden. Steam rose from kettles. The smell of frying bread and rendered fat hung thick in the air.

Every table was full.

Dockworkers. Laborers. Brick-haulers. Half-blood legbreakers who normally only showed up after dark.

Broad shoulders. Thick necks. Scar tissue and quiet eyes.

**Special breakfast deal.**

Free meal.

Might need to earn it.

Gravel stood near the bar, arms folded, expression neutral.

Crush worked the door.

When the knock came, it was polite.

Crush opened it.

**Sir Mathren Solgard** stepped in.

Full plate. Pre-dawn light just beginning to gray the edges of the street behind him.

He paused half a second inside the threshold.

Counted.

He recognized the setup instantly.

Men positioned with lines of sight.  
Benches angled.  
Clear center lane through the room.  
Hands near cudgels.

He was not offended.

He was not impressed.

He simply closed the door behind him.

The room quieted just enough to measure his footsteps as he crossed the floor.

Gravel gave him a small nod.

“Morning.”

The paladin inclined his head.

“I appreciate the hospitality.”

Gravel shrugged.

“Feeding the working class. Real charitable morning.”

Sir Mathren’s eyes moved once across the crowd.

They weren’t hostile.

They were **prepared**.

He approved of that.

If she was true, there would be no fight.

If there was a fight—

So be it.

At the far end of the room, near the hearth, **Druuna** knelt beside her small portable shrine.

A copper-and-tin sunburst polished lovingly, if imperfectly.

Her robes were clean—mostly—though mended, stained faintly at the hem from old mud and older blood.

She was speaking softly to a pair of dockhands who bowed their heads awkwardly as she finished a simple prayer.

She looked up as the room shifted.

And froze.

The paladin walked toward her.

He stopped three paces away.

Slowly, deliberately, he removed his helmet.

The face revealed was hard and weathered.

Not cruel.

But carved by years of long roads and longer nights.

A scar crossed his brow. Another tugged faintly at his jawline.

He studied her.

For a long time.

Druuna swallowed once.

Then she rose—straight-backed despite her narrow shoulders.

“I am **Druuna Sunseeker**,” she said, voice steady.  
“Disciple of **Lyosha Ohotnik**. Believer in Pelor’s mercy.”

She hesitated.

“I hold no rank in the church.”

Sir Mathren’s eyes moved to her robes.

Ragged but maintained.

To the symbol in her hand.

Battered copper-and-tin, polished with care.

Not gold.

Not silver.

**Earned.**

He straightened slightly.

“I am **Sir Mathren Solgard**, Dawn Marshal of the Third Radiant Inquiry.  
Warden of False Flame.  
Keeper of the Black Threshold.”

A murmur moved through the tavern.

Gravel’s jaw tightened.

The Dawn Marshal continued evenly.

“I am charged to root out heresy, corruption, and those who wear the Sun as a mask for greed.”

His gaze never left Druuna.

“I hunt vampires. I burn false prophets. I do not tolerate charlatans.”

The air tightened.

“If you stand in Pelor’s name falsely,” he finished calmly,  
“this will end poorly.”

---

## The Test

Druuna blinked.

Then nodded once.

“That seems fair.”

That… was not the reaction he expected.

“What are the Three Tenets of the Dawn?”

She opened her mouth confidently—

—and stopped.

Her brow furrowed.

“The warmth that drives back fear… the light that reveals truth…”

She faltered.

“And… the… um…”

Her hands tightened on the symbol.

“The third one is about… not hiding from mercy.”

That was not the phrasing.

At all.

“You cannot recite them.”

“No,” she admitted plainly.

“Then why do you preach?”

She looked at him.

Truly looked at him.

Because she didn’t understand the trap.

“Because people are hurting,” she said simply.  
“And Pelor doesn’t ask for clean hands before he gives warmth.”

That was not doctrinal language.

That was **lived** language.

“What is Pelor?”

“Hope.”

Not Light.

Not Justice.

Not Radiance.

Hope.

“What would you do if I forbade you from preaching?”

She thought about it seriously.

“I would keep helping people.”

Not defiant.

Just obvious.

“I would stop using the Sun’s name if you said I must. But I would still bring soup. And wash wounds. And sit with the dying.”

A pause.

“I don’t think Pelor would mind.”

---

## Judgment

Sir Mathren searched her face.

He looked for calculation.

He found none.

Then he did something no one expected.

He knelt.

The **Dawn Marshal of the Third Radiant Inquiry** knelt in the Blood and Sand tavern.

He gently lifted the copper-and-tin symbol in her hands.

“You are imperfect,” he said quietly.

“Yes.”

“But you are not false.”

He stood.

Turned slowly, taking in the half-blood muscle, the breakfast spread, Gravel’s guarded stance.

“You prepared for violence.”

Gravel didn’t deny it.

“She’s ours.”

The Dawn Marshal met his eyes.

“And you fed your people instead.”

Gravel shrugged.

“Two birds.”

Then Sir Mathren spoke clearly enough for the entire tavern.

“**Druuna Sunseeker is no heretic.**”

The tension broke like a snapped rope.

“You will present yourself to the nearest temple once a month for instruction,” he added.

Her face lit up.

“Truly?”

“Yes.”

He hesitated.

“And if any harm comes to you under Pelor’s banner…”

His eyes lifted to Gravel.

“…I will return.”

Not threat.

Promise.

Druuna beamed.

“Oh! You should stay for breakfast.”

The Dawn Marshal blinked once.

“I do not eat while—”

She shoved a plate into his hands before he could finish.

It was good bread.

Very good bread.

He stood there a moment, plate in hand, surrounded by half-blood legbreakers, steam rising, sunrise touching the windows.

And for the first time in a very long time—

**Sir Mathren Solgard smiled.**

Just a little.

Hope, after all, was harder to root out than heresy.
